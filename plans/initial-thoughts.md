It is great to hear you are building an application for ASHA (Accredited Social Health Activist) workers. As a Medical Officer, I can tell you firsthand that they are the absolute backbone of our public health system, especially in rural areas. However, they are incredibly overburdened with paperwork, reporting, and physical travel. An app that genuinely simplifies their workflow would be a game-changer.

To build an effective app, you need to understand that an ASHA is not a traditional 9-to-5 employee. She is a volunteer from within the community, usually a married woman, who balances her own household duties with her health activism.

Here is a breakdown of how they operate on the ground, according to the National Health Mission (NHM) guidelines and real-world practice:

1. How They Operate: The Big Picture
Time Commitment: Officially, an ASHA is expected to dedicate about 2 to 3 hours per day to her work, four to five days a week. In reality, this fluctuates wildly depending on health campaigns (like pulse polio, NCD screenings) or emergencies.

Incentive-Based: They do not receive a fixed salary. They earn task-based incentives (e.g., bringing a woman for institutional delivery, completing a child's full immunization cycle, facilitating family planning). App Insight: Tracking these incentives accurately is a massive pain point for them.

The "Triple A" Coordination: They work closely with the ANM (Auxiliary Nurse Midwife) and the AWW (Anganwadi Worker). Together, they manage the Village Health Sanitation and Nutrition Day (VHSND).

2. How They Start Their Day
An ASHA typically starts her fieldwork after finishing her own morning household chores (usually mid-morning).

Consulting the Diary/Registers: Before stepping out, she consults her ASHA diary or the various registers she is mandated to maintain (like the Village Health Register or RCH tracking lists).

Calculating Due Dates manually: She has to manually calculate who is due for what—which baby needs a vaccine today, which pregnant woman needs her iron-folic acid (IFA) tablets, and who has a delivery date approaching.

Coordination: She might make a quick call to the local ANM or Anganwadi worker to align on any joint visits or upcoming health camp duties.

3. How They Decide Whom to Visit (Prioritization)
An ASHA does not visit houses randomly. Her visits are highly targeted based on NHM protocols. She categorizes her visits based on urgency and life-cycle stages:

Highest Priority: Home Based Newborn Care (HBNC) * This is the most time-sensitive task. If a baby is born, the ASHA must visit the home on specific days to check for sepsis, weight, and feeding.

Protocol schedule: Days 1, 3, 7, 14, 21, 28, and 42 for home deliveries (institutional deliveries start at Day 3).

High Priority: Maternal Health (ANC & PNC)

Pregnant women who are due for their Antenatal Care (ANC) checkups, tetanus shots, or who are identified as High-Risk Pregnancies (HRP).

Postnatal Care (PNC) for recently delivered mothers to monitor bleeding, nutrition, and psychological well-being.

Medium Priority: Child Health & Immunization

Children due for their routine immunizations (BCG, Pentavalent, Measles, etc.). She acts as a mobilizer to ensure the mother brings the child to the vaccination site.

Routine/Campaign Priority: NCDs and Communicable Diseases

Counseling couples on family planning.

Following up with TB patients (acting as a DOTS provider).

Conducting household surveys for Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs) like hypertension and diabetes.
